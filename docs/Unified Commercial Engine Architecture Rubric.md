# Unified Commercial Engine Architecture Rubric

This rubric is the Guide's structured audit for diagnosing commercial maturity. It is intentionally written in business language. The underlying data model and implementation details are maintained separately from this doctrine.

## How to Use the Rubric

1. Review each of the five stages from the buyer's point of view.
2. Note the visible signals that support or weaken that stage.
3. Test the structural logic beneath those signals: ownership, proof, handoffs, automation, and retention discipline.
4. Place the business in the maturity tier that best matches the whole system, not just one strong asset.

## Maturity Tiers

| Score range | Tier | Meaning | Recommended next move |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 0-40 | Fragmented | activity exists, but the journey is unreliable and siloed | establish basics, clean routing, and stage clarity |
| 41-60 | Emerging | useful pieces are visible, but cohesion is still fragile | strengthen proof, ownership, and follow-through |
| 61-80 | Orchestrated | the system is becoming governable and repeatable | improve automation, context, and retention logic |
| 81-100 | Unified | the business can see and steer the journey deliberately | optimize, simplify, and protect ownership |

## Stage 1: Awareness

Observable signals:
- clear positioning across profiles, search, and owned properties
- simple owned-domain routing from social or offline touchpoints
- useful educational content that helps the buyer recognize the problem

Structural questions:
- Does the business own its main discovery paths?
- Is early attention routed somewhere purposeful?
- Is there any first-party signal capture beginning at this stage?

## Stage 2: Consideration

Observable signals:
- a website or microsite structure that supports evaluation
- proof, examples, or resources that help the buyer compare approaches
- low-friction ways to keep learning without being forced into a sales conversation

Structural questions:
- Does the business explain the problem clearly, or only describe services?
- Is follow-up structured enough to continue the conversation helpfully?
- Are the main evaluation assets aligned to the canonical journey?

## Stage 3: Decision

Observable signals:
- trust signals, FAQs, and proof relevant to the buyer's concerns
- clear calls to action and simple qualification paths
- decision support that reduces risk rather than increasing pressure

Structural questions:
- Can a buyer validate fit without unnecessary back-and-forth?
- Are objections handled consistently, or only when a strong seller happens to be involved?
- Does the team share a real definition of decision readiness?

## Stage 4: Conversion

Observable signals:
- a clean booking, proposal, payment, or contracting flow
- obvious ownership after agreement
- onboarding steps that preserve context from the pre-sale journey

Structural questions:
- Is there a reliable handoff between decision and delivery?
- Does the system create unnecessary admin at the moment of commitment?
- Can the business move from agreement to action without losing momentum?

## Stage 5: Retention

Observable signals:
- visible support, onboarding, or client guidance
- signs that value realization is monitored and reinforced
- pathways for renewal, referral, or responsible expansion

Structural questions:
- Can the business tell when an account is drifting before it becomes a churn story?
- Is customer value managed intentionally, or only reactively?
- Are retention and expansion treated as part of the same commercial design?

## Interpretation Rules

- Missing support in one stage creates drag on every stage after it.
- A polished asset does not outweigh weak governance or broken handoffs.
- The right recommendation is the next workable system improvement, not the most ambitious future-state build.
- If the current stack can support the next step, use a Managed Nervous System before recommending a deeper rebuild.
