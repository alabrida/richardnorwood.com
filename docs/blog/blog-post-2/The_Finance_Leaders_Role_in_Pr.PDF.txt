The Finance Leader’s Role in Pricing Strategy
How CFOs and financial planning and analysis teams can drive value creation through
smarter pricing.
Content Type: Article
In many companies, pricing remains an underused lever of profitability. The focus tends to tilt toward
controlling costs and driving revenue growth, with far less attention paid to how pricing decisions shape
the bottom line. Yet few actions move profit as directly as pricing does.
So why do so many finance teams get involved only after deals are done or discounts are already
granted? This late involvement limits their ability to influence outcomes when it matters most. The
opportunity lies in moving upstream. Finance leaders are well placed to help craft pricing strategies that
align with customer value and company objectives, while also promoting a more structured approach to
governance.
That influence, however, requires more than crunching numbers. Finance teams must build new
capabilities, collaborate across functions, and foster a culture where pricing is treated as a strategic tool,
not a last-minute adjustment. It also connects pricing to broader outcomes: customer satisfaction,
competitive positioning, and long-term profitability. Finance teams that embrace this broader role can
help create pricing processes that are not only more disciplined but also more responsive to changing
market conditions and customer expectations.
The following offers ideas for how finance teams can take on this role. Through lessons drawn from
software as a service (SaaS), manufacturing, consumer goods, healthcare, and services sectors, it
explores ways they can lead to smarter pricing conversations and contribute to stronger business
performance.
Scope and applicability
Mid-sized to large B2B companies—particularly in SaaS, manufacturing, healthcare, and services,
where finance teams have visibility into customer-level data and pricing discretion exists—will find this
guidance particularly applicable. While many Fortune 500 firms have pricing teams within sales or
marketing, finance teams can add unique value in environments where product bundling, margin
variability, or complex discounting patterns exist. This role is less about being the price owner and more
about enabling better decisions through cost clarity, segmentation, and scenario planning.
The untapped potential of pricing
Few business levers are as powerful as pricing, yet few are managed with consistent discipline. Even a
small price increase can deliver gains in profitability that far exceed what is achievable through cost-
cutting or volume growth. That is well known in theory but not always reflected in practice.
The reality inside many companies is more fragmented. Marketing teams often own list prices. Sales
teams manage discounts and rebates. Product teams focus on what is being sold, not how it is priced.
Finance tends to review the numbers after the fact. This division of roles leaves money on the table and
creates opportunities for misalignment.
Culture plays a role, too. Pricing is often viewed as a politically sensitive topic. Sales wants flexibility.
Marketing protects brand positioning. Product teams may be hesitant to raise prices on new features.
Without a central owner, pricing decisions can become inconsistent and reactive.
Finance can help bring structure to this environment. The goal is not to dictate price points but to make
pricing a more deliberate part of strategic planning. That means moving beyond market tolerance and
grounding pricing in an understanding of customer value, competitive realities, margin needs, and long-
term goals.
When finance brings this perspective, pricing shifts from being a tactical lever to a genuine driver of
enterprise value, with benefits that ripple across the organization.
The unique strengths finance brings to pricing
Finance teams have several natural advantages when it comes to driving better pricing outcomes,
though many organizations have yet to fully leverage these strengths.
The first is visibility. Finance can analyze margins comprehensively, from gross profit to contribution
margin and down to net profitability. Segment-level views that incorporate customer characteristics,
product lines, and sales channels provide a rich foundation for informed pricing decisions.
Finance also brings an objective lens to customer profitability. Sales organizations often focus on
revenue growth, sometimes at the expense of margin. Finance can identify truly profitable customers, as
well as those who may be generating revenue while consuming resources disproportionate to their
value. In many cases, this analysis reveals opportunities to adjust pricing strategies or to resegment
accounts for more targeted pricing approaches.
Another area where a finance department excels is in modeling price elasticity. Through historical
analysis and experimentation, financial planning and analysis (FP&A) teams can help the business
understand how pricing changes affect demand, retention, and customer acquisition. This enables
pricing decisions to move from gut feel to evidence-based strategy.
Finally, finance serves as a cross-functional partner with no sales quotas to hit or marketing narratives
to defend. This neutrality allows finance team leaders to facilitate balanced discussions about pricing.
They can help align stakeholders and ensure that pricing decisions serve both revenue growth and
margin objectives.
Building a pricing intelligence capability
To play a more proactive role in pricing, the finance team must develop capabilities that extend beyond
standard margin reporting. Several practices can help establish this foundation.
One of the most valuable practices is segment-level profitability analysis. Finance should regularly
evaluate contribution margins by customer segment, product, and channel, factoring in all relevant costs
and incentives. This level of analysis often uncovers pricing opportunities or margin leakage that would
otherwise go unnoticed.
At a global SaaS company I advised, finance identified that all customers were paying the same flat per-
seat rate, regardless of how much value they were actually receiving from the platform. Power users—
typically large enterprise clients—were accessing advanced analytics, integrations, and priority support,
yet paying the same as smaller, less active accounts. To address this, finance led a shift toward value-
based bundling—a pricing approach that packages features based on customer needs and perceived
value, rather than usage volume or internal cost. The team introduced tiered offerings, with a premium
tier tailored for high-usage clients and streamlined options for others. Within a year, the company saw a
four-point improvement in gross margin without materially impacting conversion (percentage of
qualified prospects who become paying customers)—evidence that pricing was better aligned with the
value being delivered (see Figure 1).
Price elasticity modeling is another important capability.
Historical data on past pricing changes can offer clues about how
demand responds, but controlled experiments are even more
powerful. Where feasible, finance teams should work with
marketing and product teams to design and analyze these
experiments. In subscription businesses, metrics such as net
revenue retention and customer lifetime value can provide
additional guidance.
One consumer subscription company conducted such a test with
finance’s support. The team designed a price increase for a
cohort of its most loyal customers and modeled potential churn
scenarios. The results were surprising: The customer base proved less price sensitive than expected, and
the change led to an 8% boost in net margin without significant churn.
Margin waterfall analysis is a straightforward yet powerful tool for identifying where value erodes
between list price and net realized margin. By mapping out each step—standard discounts, promotional
rebates, payment terms, and other adjustments—finance can surface margin leakage that often goes
unnoticed in aggregate reporting.
At one industrial chemicals company, finance used this approach to uncover nearly $15 million in
annual losses tied to untracked rebates and inconsistent discounting (see Figure 2). While each
adjustment seemed reasonable in isolation, the cumulative effect was significant. Once the pattern was
clear, finance partnered with sales and revenue operations to formalize discount thresholds, improve
rebate tracking, and embed margin accountability into the deal review process. These changes tightened
commercial governance and delivered meaningful improvement to overall profitability.
Finally, finance should bring pricing scenario planning into the
core planning cycle. Pricing decisions should not be treated as
static. Rather, they should be modeled alongside revenue,
margin, and retention forecasts in annual plans and rolling
updates.

Enabling cross-functional leadership
While finance teams bring critical capabilities to pricing, success depends on cross-functional
collaboration. Pricing decisions touch nearly every major function in the business.
One of the most effective ways to foster alignment is through a pricing council. This cross-functional
group, including finance, product, sales, marketing, and revenue operations, provides a forum where
pricing decisions can be evaluated and refined collaboratively. In this setting, finance can contribute the
analytical backbone while respecting the insights and priorities of other teams.
At a professional services firm I advised, such a council helped the company move past inconsistent
pricing practices. With finance leading the effort, the firm implemented discounting frameworks and
segment-specific pricing strategies that improved average deal profitability by 5 percentage points
within six months.
Quarterly pricing reviews provide another valuable mechanism. Finance can lead these sessions,
surfacing trends in realized pricing, margin outcomes, and discount patterns. Such reviews help drive
accountability and continuous improvement.
A B2B services company used this approach to identify a segment that was receiving unnecessarily
deep discounts. Through revised policies and sales team coaching, the company reduced average
discounts from 22% to 16% in two quarters, significantly improving margins.
Partnership with product teams is also essential. Finance can help quantify customer willingness to pay
(WTP)—the maximum amount different customer segments are likely to spend—and assess the
economic value of new features. This analysis informs pricing decisions that capture value without
sacrificing customer adoption.
For instance, at a cloud storage company, finance partnered with product and marketing to support the
launch of an artificial intelligence (AI)-powered search feature that allowed users to locate files using
natural language. Instead of assigning a flat price, finance helped model WTP using usage patterns,
customer interviews, and competitive benchmarks. The analysis showed that enterprise users managing
large content libraries placed a high value on the feature.
Rather than bundling it into existing plans, finance recommended introducing a new premium tier
priced 25% above the standard offering. Over the next two quarters, gross margin on upgraded accounts
rose from 55% to 63% as higher pricing was absorbed without pushback, and cost to serve (CTS)
remained flat (see Figure 3). Lower-tier plans stayed unchanged, maintaining satisfaction across the
base. Finance’s role in quantifying value and structuring the tier was key to achieving the margin lift
without impacting adoption.

Finally, finance can support sales by developing guided
discounting approaches. Rather than relying on gut feel, sales
teams can use discount ladders and playbooks grounded in
finance’s analysis of customer profitability and price sensitivity.
This approach helps sales focus on value-based selling rather
than defaulting to discounts.
Cross-industry lessons
The potential for finance to shape pricing outcomes is not
limited to any one sector. Across industries, the role of finance as a strategic partner is both applicable
and valuable.
In SaaS, finance-led modeling of consumption-based pricing has helped companies improve net
revenue retention by aligning pricing with customer value and usage patterns.
In manufacturing, finance has driven shifts from cost-plus pricing to value-based models. These
changes have often yielded substantial earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) improvements as
companies have learned to price based on customer-perceived value, not internal cost structures alone.
Consumer goods firms have relied on finance to lead elasticity analysis that informs premium
positioning and packaging decisions. A global beverage brand, for example, used finance insights to
introduce smaller, higher-margin packaging formats that captured a greater share of wallet without
eroding volume.
Healthcare organizations have leaned on finance to develop value-based care pricing models. These
efforts require sophisticated financial modeling to align incentives between payers and providers,
ensuring that pricing structures support both clinical and financial outcomes.
In professional services, finance teams have enabled shifts from hourly billing to outcome-based
contracts. Such transitions require detailed modeling of project risk, client value, and profitability.
Finance’s contribution is often the key to structuring agreements that are both attractive to clients and
sustainable for the firm.
Retailers, facing dynamic pricing environments, increasingly rely on finance to model promotional
strategies, optimize markdown pacing, and personalize pricing. Finance’s ability to track margin
impacts in real time is essential in this context.
Across all these industries, a consistent pattern emerges. When finance moves beyond retrospective
margin analysis and engages proactively in pricing strategy, the organization gains a powerful lever for
sustainable value creation. Pricing becomes not just a tactical exercise but a strategic capability.
Building pricing maturity over time
Organizations progress through stages of pricing maturity. Early on, finance may focus on margin
reporting and discount approvals. Over time, as capabilities mature, finance can lead segment
profitability analysis, elasticity modeling, and scenario planning.

The most advanced organizations treat finance as an integral partner in pricing strategy. Finance teams
in such companies develop dynamic pricing models, engage continuously with product and sales teams,
and help drive pricing decisions that align with long-term enterprise goals. Pricing in these firms is not
a periodic exercise but a living process, informed by ongoing data and market signals.
For CFOs and FP&A leaders, the first step is to assess where the organization stands today. Building
dedicated pricing analytics capabilities, investing in the right tools, and fostering cross-functional
alignment are key enablers of the journey ahead. Equally important is creating a culture where pricing
is viewed as a strategic lever, not merely a commercial tactic. As finance leaders help champion this
mindset, they position their teams to play a lasting role in shaping profitable growth.
It’s important to note that finance is rarely the sole owner of pricing strategy—nor should it be. In large
organizations with dedicated pricing, sales, or marketing teams, finance’s value lies in enhancing
discipline, surfacing tradeoffs, and quantifying pricing impacts—not in setting prices unilaterally. The
extent of finance’s leadership depends on the company’s structure, maturity, and business model.
Conclusion
Pricing is not just a lever for profitability. It is a reflection of how well a company understands its
customers, its markets, and the value it delivers. In too many businesses, pricing is still fragmented
across functions and too often driven by instinct rather than insight.
Finance leaders are in a unique position to help change that dynamic. They have the analytical tools, the
cross-functional visibility, and the financial discipline to guide more deliberate pricing decisions. When
finance steps forward and leads, pricing evolves from a tactical activity to a true strategic capability.
For finance teams, this shift does not happen overnight. It requires building new capabilities, forging
stronger partnerships with product and sales, and developing a deeper understanding of how pricing
shapes customer behavior and profitability.
But the payoff is well worth the effort. Companies that master pricing will not only perform better
financially, they will build stronger, more resilient relationships with their customers. Finance should
help lead the way.
